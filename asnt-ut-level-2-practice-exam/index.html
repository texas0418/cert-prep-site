Question 1 · calculations

A probe operates at 2.25 MHz in steel. What is the longitudinal wavelength?

Answer: 0.103 in
Lambda = V / f using steel longitudinal 0.232 in/us: 0.232 / 2.25 = 0.103 in.
Question 2 · calculations

A 2.25 MHz probe generates a shear wave in steel. What is the shear wavelength?

Answer: 0.056 in
Lambda = V / f using steel shear 0.127 in/us: 0.127 / 2.25 = 0.056 in.
Question 3 · calculations

A 5 MHz longitudinal probe is used in steel. What is the wavelength?

Answer: 0.046 in
Lambda = 0.232 / 5 = 0.046 in.
Question 4 · physics

Shear waves cannot propagate through:

Answer: Liquids and gases
Liquids and gases have no shear stiffness, so they cannot sustain transverse (shear) waves.
Question 5 · physics

The particle motion of a Rayleigh (surface) wave is:

Answer: Elliptical
Rayleigh waves move surface particles in an elliptical orbit combining longitudinal and transverse components.
Question 6 · physics

A Rayleigh wave penetrates to a depth of approximately:

Answer: One wavelength
Rayleigh wave energy concentrates within roughly one wavelength of the surface.
Question 7 · equipment

Pulse repetition rate (PRR) is:

Answer: The number of pulses the instrument transmits per second
PRR is how many separate ultrasonic pulses are fired each second.
Question 8 · equipment

A B-scan presents:

Answer: A cross-sectional (side) view showing reflector depth versus probe position
A B-scan builds a side-view profile of the part, mapping depth against scan position.
Question 9 · calibration

A distance-sensitivity calibration (DSC) block is used to:

Answer: Calibrate distance and sensitivity for angle-beam inspection
The DSC block combines distance and sensitivity references for angle-beam setup.
Question 10 · calibration

A calibration block should be made of material that:

Answer: Has acoustic properties similar to the part being tested
Velocity, attenuation, and surface condition of the block should match the test piece for valid calibration.

Question 11 · calibration

Straight-beam range calibration is commonly performed using:

Answer: Multiple back-wall echoes from a known thickness or a step block
Back-wall multiples from a known thickness give evenly spaced echoes to calibrate the sweep.
Question 12 · techniques

On an A-scan, a lamination in plate is typically indicated by:

Answer: A reduced back-wall echo with an intermediate echo from the lamination
The lamination reflects sound early, raising an intermediate echo at its own depth (often near mid-wall in rolled plate, but wherever the lamination lies) and stealing energy from the back wall.
Question 13 · techniques

The back-reflection (loss of back wall) technique detects flaws by:

Answer: Noting a drop in back-wall amplitude when sound is intercepted by a flaw
A flaw that blocks or scatters the beam reduces the back-wall echo, signaling its presence.
Question 14 · techniques

Welds are commonly inspected with angle-beam probes because:

Answer: Weld flaws are often not parallel to the surface and the crown blocks straight-beam access
Angle beams reach the fusion faces and root at favorable angles and scan from beside the reinforcement.
Question 15 · flaw detection

A lamination is best described as:

Answer: A planar separation in the base metal lying parallel to the surface
Laminations are flat, base-metal separations parallel to the rolling surface, found with straight-beam testing.
Question 16 · flaw detection

Porosity typically produces:

Answer: Many small, scattered, lower-amplitude indications
Gas pores act as numerous small reflectors, giving a scattered, low-level response.
Question 17 · flaw detection

Slag inclusions are generally:

Answer: Irregular volumetric reflectors with variable amplitude
Trapped slag forms irregular volumetric pockets that reflect sound from many directions with varying strength.
Question 18 · codes api1104

Under API 1104, the acceptance criteria for a discontinuity are established by:

Answer: The governing code, applied per the method and criteria it specifies
Acceptance criteria are defined by the governing code and applied using the method and limits it specifies, not chosen by the operator, manufacturer, or crew.
Question 19 · codes api1104

The alternative acceptance criteria in API 1104 generally require:

Answer: Stress analysis and reliable flaw sizing to assess fitness for service
Fitness-for-service acceptance depends on stress levels, material toughness, and accurate flaw dimensions, so reliable sizing is essential.
Question 20 · general practice safety

A UT technician working under API 1104 is responsible for:

Answer: Performing the examination to the qualified procedure and reporting results accurately
The technician's duty is faithful execution of the qualified procedure and honest, accurate reporting.

UT Level II exam FAQ

What's on the UT Level II written exam?

Employer-administered Level II exams under SNT-TC-1A typically include a general exam (UT physics, equipment, calibration, techniques) and a specific exam based on the employer's procedures and applicable codes. Content varies by employer and specification — always study to your written practice.

Are the calculation questions in imperial or metric?

Both. The app lets you toggle every dual-unit question between imperial and metric; the samples on this page use imperial units.

Is this an official ASNT resource?

No. These are independent practice questions reviewed by subject-matter reviewers. They are not actual exam questions, and this site is not affiliated with or endorsed by ASNT.

Does the app cover other NDT methods?

The architecture supports additional method modules (PT, MT, RT, PAUT and more) that roll out as their banks pass review — UT Level II conventional is the flagship module today.
